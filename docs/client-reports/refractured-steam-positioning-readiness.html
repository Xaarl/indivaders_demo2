Client report | Steam positioning readiness

Refractured is ready for positioning discipline, not public Steam positioning.

Indievaders turns the current private combat prototype into a demo-readiness brief: what the game can credibly promise, what expectations it should avoid creating, and what must be proven before a Steam page, trailer, or public share copy exists.

Verdict

The evidence supports a focused dark 2.5D combat-readability lane. It does not yet support broad action-game, open 3D, launch-readiness, or content-depth claims.

Executive Summary

Use the report before the Steam page exists.

The useful output now is a pre-Steam promise sheet, a demo-readiness gate list, a risk map, and sprint priorities tied to current proof.

Keep the public claim narrow.

Benchmark for lessons, not forecasts.

Comparable games clarify the trap, not the target

These are reference comparables for positioning and expectation-setting. They are not commercial comps, production forecasts, or scope promises.

Sifu

Reported

Safe lesson: defensive timing, enemy pressure, and environmental combat must be readable before the promise expands.

Unsafe assumption: Refractured should not imply Sifu-level authored combat density, animation polish, or mastery depth yet.

Midnight Fight Express

Confirmed

Unsafe assumption: do not borrow its mission count, weapon breadth, cosmetics, or mo-cap polish as near-term scope.

Absolver

Confirmed

Safe lesson: deep combat-system language quickly creates expectations for mastery and customization.

Unsafe assumption: avoid open-world, RPG, multiplayer, or combat-deck language unless those systems are scoped and demonstrable.

Streets of Rage 4

Confirmed

Safe lesson: beat-em-up players expect immediate impact, legible enemy waves, and clear stage pressure.

Unsafe assumption: do not promise co-op, arcade revival structure, or franchise-scale polish.

Fight'N Rage

Confirmed

Safe lesson: responsive controls and compact mastery pressure matter more than raw feature count.

Unsafe assumption: Refractured does not need to become a 2D arcade-route game or promise local co-op.

Five production risks decide whether the promise lands

Animation intake

Known retargeting and promotion gates mean demo-critical clips must pass lab and runtime review before public use.

First-hit feel

Players will judge impact and responsiveness before they understand the larger combat system.

Camera readability

Controlled 3D/2.5D combat depends on threat direction, spacing, recovery, and enemy staging being legible.

Scope drift

Comparable games imply weapons, levels, co-op, online systems, and mastery depth that are not yet evidenced.

Performance claims

Public performance language should wait until the demo slice is measured on target-like hardware.

Recommended Next Steps

1 Revalidate the combat baseline

Review one attack, one defensive response, one enemy pressure pattern, and one reset loop. Output: pass/fail note for first-hit feel, defense clarity, recovery, and repeatability.

2 Tighten first-minute proof

Curate one readable encounter that shows pressure, impact, defense, and camera clarity without feature explanation. Output: first-minute checklist and capture target.

3 Write a one-page Steam promise before building a page

Define the player fantasy, supported claims, deferred claims, and evidence labels. Output: a team-approved promise sheet.

4 Lock demo-critical animation rules

Identify the minimum promoted clip set and reject clips that fail source, retarget, root motion, return pose, combat speed, lab review, or runtime checks.

5 Validate camera and encounter readability together

Review camera framing, enemy spacing, threat direction, hit reaction, and recovery timing in the same encounter.

6 Define the performance claim boundary

Measure the demo slice on target-like hardware and document what was tested, what was not, and what should not be claimed yet.

Open Questions Before Public Positioning

What is the first public slice?

The report assumes demo-readiness is the next useful milestone. A different target, such as publisher pitch or combat prototype grant, would shift wording.

Which clips are truly demo-critical?

The animation policy is clear, but the minimum public-facing clip set still needs a pass/fail list.

What hardware claim is safe?

Older PC and Steam Deck-class constraints are documented, but public performance language needs slice-level validation first.
