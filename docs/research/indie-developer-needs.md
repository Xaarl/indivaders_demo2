# Indie Developer Needs

This note captures working product assumptions for Indievaders. It is grounded in the current product direction and should be validated through customer conversations, early report delivery, and observed behavior in the prototype.

## Core Needs

Indie developers do not need a generic dashboard first. They need help turning public market context into decisions under time, budget, and attention constraints.

The strongest needs are:

- Choosing realistic comparables.
- Avoiding fantasy benchmarks.
- Understanding what nearby players praise or punish.
- Deciding what the Steam page should promise first.
- Checking whether price, scope, and visible proof align.
- Preparing for demos, Next Fest, creator outreach, publisher conversations, and launch.
- Knowing which unknowns matter instead of filling them with guesses.

## Common Failure Modes

Wrong reference set:

- The team picks famous games that share surface traits but not scale, license, audience awareness, publisher context, or content breadth.

Tag and promise drift:

- The Steam page uses broad tags and feature lists that create expectations the demo cannot satisfy.

Review-risk blindness:

- The team reads reviews as praise/complaint lists instead of expectation signals that can guide demo and page decisions.

Production-reality mismatch:

- The team copies scope from games that look indie but are backed by larger resources.

Price/scope mismatch:

- The visible page does not prove enough depth, replayability, or polish for the intended price.

Outreach too early:

- Creator or press outreach starts before the page communicates the hook clearly.

## Buying Triggers

Likely buying moments:

- Steam page draft is live or nearly live.
- Demo is being prepared.
- Next Fest deadline is approaching.
- Price is being debated.
- Team is choosing reference games for positioning.
- Publisher/creator outreach is being prepared.
- Early feedback suggests players misunderstand the hook.

## Jobs To Be Done

When I am preparing my Steam page, help me identify which games I should actually compare against so I do not set the wrong expectations.

When I am preparing a demo, help me understand which nearby review complaints could become my review risks.

When I am choosing a price, help me see whether my visible page proves the scope that price implies.

When I am planning outreach, help me turn my game's hook into creator angles that are specific enough to pitch.

When I am revisiting the market later, help me see what changed and whether it affects my page, demo, price, or launch plan.

## Research Questions To Validate

- Which teams will pay before they have a Steam page versus only after a page exists?
- How much manual expert review is required for the report to feel trustworthy?
- Which section creates the strongest purchase intent: comparables, review risks, price/scope, or production reality?
- Does "workspace" increase perceived value before persistence exists, or does it create confusion?
- How often would teams want market refreshes: weekly, before milestones, or only near launch events?
- Which evidence labels are clear without explanation?

## Signals To Watch In Early Customers

- Do users click the sample report before the intake?
- Which report sections get mentioned in replies?
- Do users provide reference games voluntarily?
- Do users ask for review-risk analysis or mostly competitor analysis?
- Do users want the workspace after receiving the report?
- Do users ask for refreshes unprompted?
