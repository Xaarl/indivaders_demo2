# Product Definition

## One-Sentence Definition

Indievaders sells evidence-labeled Steam positioning reports that help indie game teams choose real comparables, avoid unsafe benchmarks, translate nearby player reactions into design and launch decisions, and decide what to change before public attention arrives.

## Audience

Primary audience:

- Serious solo developers and small indie teams.
- Teams preparing a Steam page, demo, Next Fest beat, creator outreach, publisher pitch, or launch.
- Developers who already have references in mind but do not know which ones are commercially useful versus misleading.

Secondary audience later:

- Small publishers and consultants supporting multiple indie games.
- Teams with one shipped game that need post-launch market tracking.

## What Indievaders Sells Now

The first sellable product is the `$49 early Steam Positioning Report`.

It should be sold as a focused decision report, not as raw data access or a giant SaaS. The value is not "more information." The value is an outside read that turns public market signals into decisions the team can act on.

The paid report should answer:

- What does this game really compete with?
- Which attractive references are unsafe benchmarks?
- Which review patterns from nearby games create design/page risks?
- What production reality changes the comparison?
- Does the visible game promise support the intended price and scope?
- What should the team change first?

## What Indievaders Becomes Later

The long-term product is a private project workspace for one game, built around a report that can refresh over time.

The progression is:

1. Interactive report.
2. Private project workspace.
3. Refreshable market monitoring.
4. Subscription intelligence layer.

The subscription should not be sold until the report is valuable enough that teams want to keep it alive.

## Product Ladder

| Surface | Purpose | User Question | Product Boundary |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Landing | Explain the offer and earn attention | "Is this for my Steam/game problem?" | No full methodology wall, no fake SaaS dashboard |
| Interactive sample report | Prove the report logic | "Can this catch mistakes I would miss?" | Public sample only, not a custom report |
| Email sample brief | Capture qualified interest | "Can I get a compact example later?" | Short teaser, not the main paid value |
| Early report intake | Collect minimum project context | "Can I request my own report?" | No fake instant generation or fake payment certainty |
| Paid report | Deliver custom decisions | "What should I change for my game?" | Evidence-labeled, source-backed, private |
| Private workspace | Hold report, actions, comparables, and sources | "Where do I work from this report?" | Real persistence required before selling as workspace |
| Market Watch | Refresh tracked signals | "What changed since the report?" | Future subscription layer, not current proof |

## What Belongs In The Sample Report

The public interactive sample report should show the product's reasoning style:

- A small fictional/representative indie project.
- 3-5 verdicts.
- A comparable map with safe lessons and unsafe assumptions.
- Production-reality checks.
- Review-risk translation from player signal to design/action.
- Price/scope bands without revenue forecasts.
- 3-5 next actions.
- A source log with evidence labels.

The sample should create one clear conclusion: "This is not a PDF. It helps me change decisions."

## What Belongs In The Paid Report

The paid report should be specific to the customer's game:

- Executive read.
- Market lane.
- True comparables and rejected benchmarks.
- Production reality.
- Tag and positioning recommendations.
- Review risk map.
- Price and scope sanity.
- Launch timing notes.
- Creator starter angles.
- Prioritized next actions.
- Source appendix.

Every important recommendation needs an evidence trail or explicit inference label.

## What Belongs In The Workspace

The workspace should only contain objects a customer would return to:

- Project profile.
- Report snapshot.
- Comparable board.
- Review risk map.
- Production reality.
- Action board.
- Source log.
- Later: market watch refreshes.

Do not turn the workspace into a general analytics dashboard. It should remain organized around one game's positioning decisions.

## What Belongs In Market Monitoring

Market monitoring should track changes that alter decisions:

- New nearby games entering the lane.
- Comparable games changing price, discount behavior, tags, trailers, demos, or review profile.
- New review-risk language that affects demo/page expectations.
- Steam event timing relevant to the project.
- Creator/community angle changes if sourceable.

It should not start as a raw feed. It should answer: "What changed, why does it matter, and what should I reconsider?"

## What Should Wait

- Full subscription dashboard.
- Raw searchable database.
- AI chat/copilot.
- Revenue estimates as a central selling point.
- Creator CRM.
- Payment automation before the early report flow is validated.
- Multi-project agency/publisher workspaces.
